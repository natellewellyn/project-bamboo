# EL Educator Briefing: Who Are Our Customers?

*Prepared for CEO meeting — March 24, 2026*

---

## The Three Roles at a Glance

English Learner programs in K-12 districts are staffed by three distinct roles that form a hierarchy. Each has very different day-to-day work, pain points, and relationships to software products. Understanding the distinctions matters because the person who *buys* a product like this is rarely the person who *uses* it most.

| | ELD Teacher | EL Specialist / Coach | EL Director |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Scope** | Classroom / caseload | School or cluster of schools | District-wide |
| **Core job** | Teach English to students | Support teachers, coordinate programs | Strategy, compliance, budget |
| **Relationship to data** | Produces it (assessments, grades) | Analyzes and acts on it | Reports on it to state/feds |
| **Buying power** | Almost none | Influences purchases | Makes or approves purchases |
| **Typical count per district** | Many (1 per ~25-35 EL students) | A few (1 per school or cluster) | 1-2 per district |

## Role 1: ELD Teachers

### What they do

ELD (English Language Development) teachers provide direct English language instruction to students classified as English Learners. Depending on the district's program model, they might:

- **Pull-out model:** Pull small groups of EL students from general education classes for dedicated English instruction (30-60 min/day)
- **Push-in / co-teaching model:** Go into general education classrooms and support EL students alongside the classroom teacher
- **Self-contained ELD class:** Teach a dedicated ELD period, especially at the secondary level (middle/high school)
- **Newcomer programs:** Work with recently arrived students who have very limited English, often in an intensive setting

Their day is packed with small-group instruction, lesson planning differentiated across multiple proficiency levels (a single class might have students at WIDA levels 1 through 4), and constant context-switching between grade levels and content areas.

### Key pain points

**Time scarcity is the dominant theme.** ELD teachers rarely have enough time with each student. In a pull-out model, a teacher might see 60-80+ students across the week in 30-minute blocks. They're also expected to collaborate with general education teachers, attend IEP meetings for dually-identified students, and do their own assessment and progress monitoring.

Other pain points include:

- **Differentiation overload.** A single group of 6 students might span 3 proficiency levels and 2 grade levels. Planning meaningful instruction for each is exhausting.
- **Isolation.** ELD teachers are often the only one in their building, with no peer to plan with or learn from. They may feel like an afterthought in the school's culture.
- **Assessment burden.** Beyond daily formative assessment, they administer (or help administer) the annual ACCESS for ELLs assessment (the WIDA consortium's proficiency test), interim benchmarks, and often Flashlight's own assessments. Each generates data that needs to go somewhere.
- **Lack of curriculum.** Many districts don't provide a dedicated ELD curriculum. Teachers cobble together materials from multiple sources.
- **General education buy-in.** Classroom teachers sometimes view EL students as "the ELD teacher's responsibility" rather than a shared obligation. This creates friction, especially in push-in/co-teaching models.

### Relationship to technology

ELD teachers are **heavy users but not buyers**. They interact with:

- **Student Information System (SIS):** PowerSchool, Infinite Campus — to look up student info, enter grades
- **Assessment platforms:** Flashlight (for benchmarks/assignments), plus whatever the state uses for ACCESS
- **A data/compliance platform (if the district has one):** This is where Ellevation or a similar tool lives — teachers look up student proficiency levels, see recommended strategies, maybe log services
- **General classroom tools:** Google Classroom, Canvas, etc.

The key insight: ELD teachers don't want more software. They want fewer places to go to find what they need. If they have to log into 4 systems to figure out a student's proficiency level, home language, accommodations, and assessment history, that's a problem.

## Role 2: EL Specialists / EL Coaches / EL Coordinators

*(Title varies widely by district — may also be called EL Program Specialist, Bilingual Coach, ELL Coordinator, ESOL Lead, etc.)*

### What they do

EL specialists sit between the classroom and the district office. They are the connective tissue of the EL program. A typical week includes:

- **Coaching general education teachers** on sheltered instruction strategies (SIOP, GLAD, etc.) — how to make grade-level content accessible to EL students
- **Analyzing data** — looking at ACCESS scores, benchmark results, and reclassification rates to identify struggling students, effective practices, and compliance gaps
- **Managing the reclassification process** — determining which students have met criteria to exit EL services (this is high-stakes and heavily regulated)
- **Coordinating with ELD teachers** — ensuring instructional minutes are met, programs are aligned, new students are properly identified and placed
- **Running the Home Language Survey and identification process** — when a new student enrolls, someone has to review the survey, determine if screening is needed, administer the screener (WIDA Screener or state equivalent), and place the student
- **Compliance documentation** — ensuring the district can demonstrate it's meeting Title III and ESSA requirements (parent notification letters, service delivery logs, assessment records)
- **Leading or organizing professional development** for school staff on EL strategies

### Key pain points

**Data fragmentation is the #1 problem.** The specialist needs to pull together information from multiple systems — the SIS has demographics, the assessment platform has scores, the compliance system (if one exists) has service logs, and sometimes critical information lives in spreadsheets or paper files. They spend enormous time on data wrangling that should be automated.

Other pain points:

- **Compliance paperwork.** Federal and state reporting requirements are extensive. Title III requires tracking things like: how many EL students were identified, how many received services, what percentage made progress on ACCESS, what percentage were reclassified, whether reclassified students were monitored for 4 years afterward. Gathering this data manually is brutal.
- **Limited authority.** Specialists often have responsibility without formal authority. They coach teachers but can't require them to change practice. They identify program problems but may not control the budget to fix them.
- **Reclassification complexity.** Criteria vary by state but often involve multiple measures (ACCESS score, teacher input, grades, parent consultation). Managing this process for dozens or hundreds of students is a logistical challenge.
- **Principal buy-in.** School principals may not prioritize EL programs, especially in schools with small EL populations. The specialist has to advocate constantly.

### Relationship to technology

EL specialists are the **power users** of platforms like Ellevation. They're the ones who need a unified dashboard showing student data, assessment results, compliance status, and reclassification progress all in one place. They're also the ones who feel the pain most acutely when that unified view doesn't exist.

They are strong **influencers** in purchasing decisions. The EL director often asks them what tools they need.

## Role 3: EL Directors / EL Program Administrators

*(May be called Director of Multilingual Programs, Director of Language Acquisition, EL Program Manager, Title III Coordinator, etc.)*

### What they do

The EL director is the senior district leader responsible for the entire English Learner program. In smaller districts, this might be one person wearing many hats (sometimes combined with other roles like Special Ed director). In larger districts, it's a full-time position with a small team.

Their work is strategic and compliance-driven:

- **Program design and oversight** — choosing the instructional model(s) the district will use, setting goals, monitoring outcomes
- **Budget management** — administering Title III funds (federal), state EL funding if applicable, and advocating for general fund support
- **State and federal compliance** — ensuring the district meets all ESSA/Title III requirements, completing consolidated state applications, responding to audits or monitoring visits
- **Hiring and staffing** — recruiting qualified ELD teachers (a chronic shortage area), determining caseload ratios
- **Vendor and partner management** — selecting and managing contracts with companies like Flashlight, Ellevation, curriculum providers, assessment vendors
- **Board and community relations** — presenting EL program outcomes to the school board, engaging with immigrant/refugee community organizations, managing parent advisory committees (required under Title III)
- **Data and accountability** — tracking program-level metrics: ACCESS growth, reclassification rates, long-term English learner (LTEL) rates, academic achievement gaps

### Key pain points

**Accountability with inadequate resources.** EL directors are accountable for outcomes but chronically under-resourced. Title III funding provides roughly $100-150 per EL student at the federal level — a fraction of what's needed. They have to do more with less, constantly.

Other pain points:

- **Reporting burden.** State and federal reporting requirements are extensive and time-consuming. Many directors spend weeks each year compiling data for state reports, Title III applications, and compliance documentation.
- **Data visibility.** Directors need district-wide dashboards showing program health — how many students at each proficiency level, progress toward reclassification, compliance status at each school. Getting this view often requires manually aggregating data from multiple sources.
- **Political navigation.** EL programs exist in a political context. Board members, parents, and community groups have opinions. Budget decisions pit EL programs against other priorities. Immigration politics can spill into school board meetings.
- **Staff quality and retention.** Finding certified ELD/ESL teachers is a national challenge. Directors deal with high turnover, the need for alternative certifications, and the challenge of training general education teachers to support ELs.
- **Long-term English learners (LTELs).** Students who have been in EL programs for 5+ years without reclassifying are a growing concern. Directors face pressure to address this but the solutions are complex and resource-intensive.

### Relationship to technology

EL directors are the **buyers and decision-makers** for products like Ellevation and (potentially) Flashlight's new offering. They care about:

- Reducing the compliance reporting burden
- Getting district-wide data visibility without manual aggregation
- Demonstrating program effectiveness to the board and state
- Integration with existing systems (SIS, assessment platforms, rostering)
- Cost relative to Title III and general fund budgets
- Ease of adoption (they can't spend months training staff)

## The Compliance Landscape

This is the regulatory backdrop that drives much of the work (and technology purchasing) for these roles.

### Federal requirements (ESSA / Title III)

- **Identification:** Districts must identify all potential EL students via a Home Language Survey and screen them for English proficiency within 30 days of enrollment.
- **Services:** Districts must provide "language instruction educational programs" (LIEPs) to all identified EL students. The specific model is a local choice, but services must be provided.
- **Assessment:** Annual English proficiency assessment is mandatory. Most states use WIDA ACCESS for ELLs (36 states + territories) or ELPAC (California). Results must be reported to the state.
- **Reclassification:** Districts must establish criteria for when a student is reclassified as "proficient" and exits EL services. Reclassified students must be monitored for 4 years.
- **Parent notification:** Parents must be notified within 30 days of identification, with specific information about their child's proficiency level, program placement, and right to opt out.
- **Title III funding:** Competitive/formula-based federal funding for supplemental EL services. Comes with additional reporting requirements.

### State-level variation

States add their own layer. Examples of how this varies:

- **California:** Has its own assessment (ELPAC instead of ACCESS), specific reclassification criteria, and "English Learner Roadmap" policy framework
- **Texas:** Extensive LPAC (Language Proficiency Assessment Committee) requirements, bilingual education mandates for districts with 20+ ELs in a grade
- **New York:** Specific requirements for "Units of Study" in ENL (English as a New Language), mandated time allocations based on proficiency level

This state variation is why districts need flexible software — a one-size-fits-all compliance tool won't work.

## How Ellevation Fits Into This World

Ellevation Education is the most prominent dedicated EL data management platform in K-12 education. Understanding their product is critical because they're the primary competitive benchmark (and, per your project context, a current Flashlight partner turning competitor).

### What Ellevation does

Ellevation provides a centralized platform for managing EL student data, compliance workflows, and instructional support. Their core products include:

- **Ellevation Platform** — The main data hub. Ingests student data from the SIS and assessment systems, provides dashboards and reports, tracks compliance workflows (identification, reclassification, monitoring), and generates required parent notification letters.
- **Ellevation Strategies** — A library of research-based instructional strategies matched to students' proficiency levels. Designed for general education teachers (not just ELD specialists) to make content accessible to ELs. This is their "expand beyond compliance" play.

### Who uses it and how

- **EL Directors** use it for district-wide dashboards, compliance reporting, and demonstrating program health to the board/state.
- **EL Specialists** use it daily — managing reclassification workflows, looking up student data, coaching teachers with the Strategies tool.
- **ELD Teachers** use it to look up student proficiency data and access strategies, but it's not their primary instructional tool.
- **General education teachers** may use the Strategies tool if the district has purchased it and pushed adoption.

### Ellevation's strengths (from the market perspective)

- First-mover in dedicated EL data management — many districts have used them for years
- Strong compliance workflow automation (especially reclassification)
- The Strategies tool differentiates them from pure data platforms
- Integration with major SIS platforms
- Brand recognition among EL directors

### Ellevation's likely weaknesses (hypotheses to validate)

- **Assessment content is not their strength.** They manage data about assessments but don't create the assessments themselves — that's Flashlight's domain.
- **Price.** Dedicated EL platforms can be expensive relative to Title III budgets. Smaller districts may not be able to justify the cost.
- **Integration depth.** How well do they actually integrate with Flashlight's assessment data? If the connection is clunky, that's a gap.
- **General ed teacher adoption.** Strategies is only valuable if teachers actually use it. Adoption is a perennial challenge.

## How Flashlight Fits In Today

Based on your project context, Flashlight currently serves these educators in a specific but narrow way:

- **Flashlight creates assessment content** — Benchmarks (universally used by districts) and Assignments (teacher-created formative assessments, less universally adopted).
- **Flashlight packages and sends data** from these assessments to partners like Ellevation and EduSkills, who handle the reporting, progress tracking, and compliance layers.
- **Flashlight's current relationship is primarily with ELD teachers** (who administer assessments) and **EL directors** (who purchase the product), but the data management and compliance value goes to partner platforms.

The strategic implication is clear: Flashlight owns the assessment content and has the customer relationships, but gives away the data layer — which is where the stickiest, highest-value workflows live (compliance, reclassification, reporting). The new product aims to capture that value.

## Key Questions for Your CEO Meeting

These are the questions this research raises that would be worth discussing:

1. **Which role is the primary buyer vs. the primary user?** EL directors buy, but specialists and teachers use the product daily. How do you serve both?

2. **What's the entry point?** Do you lead with compliance/reporting (director pain point) or with a better data experience around Flashlight's own assessments (specialist/teacher pain point)?

3. **How do existing Flashlight customers handle compliance today?** Of the 200+ customers, how many use Ellevation? EduSkills? Spreadsheets? Nothing? The answer shapes the competitive landscape.

4. **What's the switching cost from Ellevation?** Districts with years of historical data in Ellevation may resist switching. Is the play to replace Ellevation entirely, or to offer a "good enough" compliance layer that's bundled with the assessment data they already own?

5. **State variation strategy.** Compliance requirements vary significantly by state. Which states are Flashlight's 200+ customers concentrated in? That determines which compliance rules to support first.

6. **The Strategies angle.** Ellevation's Strategies tool is their moat beyond compliance. Does Flashlight have an equivalent play? The assessment content expertise could potentially fuel an instructional support feature that's more powerful than Ellevation's.

## Glossary of Terms You'll Hear

- **ACCESS for ELLs** — Annual English proficiency assessment used in 36 WIDA-member states. Scores range from 1.0 to 6.0 across four domains (listening, speaking, reading, writing).
- **EL / ELL / ML** — English Learner / English Language Learner / Multilingual Learner. Different terms for the same population. "ML" and "multilingual" are increasingly preferred.
- **ELD / ESL / ENL** — English Language Development / English as a Second Language / English as a New Language. Different names for the instructional program. The name varies by state.
- **ELPAC** — English Language Proficiency Assessments for California. California's alternative to ACCESS.
- **ESSA** — Every Student Succeeds Act. Federal law governing K-12 education, including Title III (EL programs).
- **Home Language Survey (HLS)** — Form given to all enrolling families. If a language other than English is indicated, the student must be screened.
- **LIEP** — Language Instruction Educational Program. The formal name for whatever EL service model a district uses.
- **LTEL** — Long-Term English Learner. A student who has been in EL services for 5+ years. This is a problem indicator.
- **Reclassification (reclass)** — The process of moving a student out of EL status when they demonstrate English proficiency. Criteria vary by state.
- **RFEP** — Reclassified Fluent English Proficient. The status of a student who has been reclassified out of EL services.
- **SIOP / GLAD** — Sheltered Instruction Observation Protocol / Guided Language Acquisition Design. Popular instructional frameworks for making content accessible to ELs.
- **Title III** — The section of ESSA that specifically addresses EL education and provides supplemental federal funding.
- **WIDA** — The consortium that develops the ACCESS assessment and ELD standards used in 36+ states.
